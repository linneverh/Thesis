\section{The seafood disclosure frontier: evidence from six large
reporters}\label{cohort-analysis}

This chapter answers RQ1. Its purpose is to establish, empirically and
from primary documents, what mature European seafood processors actually
disclose --- and therefore the practical \emph{upper bound} of sector
disclosure depth against which the demands mapped in later %LI: previoius no - chapter 5? chapters, and
the expectations placed on smaller firms, must be read. The cohort is not
a picture of typical practice; it is a picture of the best-in-class frontier.

\subsection{The frontier as a disclosure-maturity
question}\label{cohort-theory}

Corporate sustainability disclosure is not adopted all at once but develops
through stages of organisational maturity: firms move from ad-hoc, reactive
reporting toward systematic, integrated disclosure as capability accumulates
(Baumgartner \& Ebner, 2010), and the completeness and quality of a firm's
reporting can be read as a position on that maturity gradient (C\"oster et al.,
2020). The most advanced reporters in a sector therefore mark its practical
\emph{frontier}: the upper bound of what is currently feasible to disclose given
real data systems and resources. %LI: of what is feasible but also relevant no - reference to Double Materiality? or not yet here but mentioning of relevant! 

Two features make that frontier analytically useful here. First, where leading
firms converge on the same disclosures \emph{despite} differing business models,
that convergence signals institutional pressure rather than idiosyncratic choice
--- the mimetic and normative isomorphism by which practices harden into sector
table stakes (DiMaggio \& Powell, 1983). %LI: not understood what the point is - cleareer language please. 
 A universal core, if one exists, is thus
the sector's \emph{revealed} consensus on what must be reported, not merely an
average. Second, the frontier calibrates the realistic ambition for a
resource-limited firm: if even the best-resourced reporters leave requirements
uncovered, completeness is the wrong yardstick for an SME, and the question
becomes which subset of disclosures matters most --- the question the demand
mapping in the following chapter takes up.

Existing work characterises sustainability-reporting maturity in general terms
and describes seafood sustainability governance at the level of the movement and
its institutions %LI: which literature - source? 
but does not map, at disclosure-requirement level, what the
sector's leading firms actually report. This chapter supplies that map and reads
it for three things: the height of the frontier, the universal core beneath it,
and the sector's collective blind spots. %LI: translate this into plain language - what does it mean, eg the height??

One feature of the cohort must be stated plainly at the outset, because it bounds
every claim that follows: the cohort is a purposive \emph{upper-bound} sample. It
establishes what is empirically feasible to disclose in this sector %LI: this is an assumption though! , not what a

\subsection{Data and method}\label{cohort-method}

\textbf{Data and scope.} The primary sources are the firms' own published
sustainability reports: the most recent report for each firm, from financial
year~2024 and, where already published, 2025 (the year-over-year comparison later
in the chapter uses the three firms for which both are available), supplemented
where available by each firm's CSRD-aligned ESRS-index appendix. Each report was
read and coded \emph{by hand} against the \emph{topical} ESRS standards --- the
ten environmental, social and governance standards (E1--E5, S1--S4, G1) and their
disclosure requirements. The two cross-cutting standards, ESRS~1 and ESRS~2
(general principles and general disclosures), are deliberately out of scope: they
govern \emph{how} a firm reports rather than \emph{which} sustainability topics it
addresses, and it is topical coverage that this chapter measures.

\textbf{Unit of analysis: material topics, not IROs.} Coding is at the level of
the ESRS topical \emph{disclosure requirements} (DRs) --- the material
sustainability topics a report addresses --- and deliberately not at the level of
the firm-specific impacts, risks and opportunities (IROs) that sit beneath a
double-materiality assessment. Three reasons make the DR the right grain. First,
IROs are entity-specific: each firm's materiality process yields its own impacts,
risks and opportunities, so they are not comparable across firms and cannot be
aggregated into a cross-firm coverage measure. Second, the question here is one of
\emph{breadth} --- which topics a mature reporter addresses at all --- for which
presence at DR level is the natural unit; IROs speak to a firm's internal
prioritisation, not to the sector's disclosure frontier. Third, only three of the
six firms report under the ESRS, and the GRI reporters produce no ESRS-style IROs
at all, so the topical DR is the only common denominator on which all six firms
can be placed. %LI: are these actually relevant - especially first one - if yes source ESRS guidance or something! 

\textbf{Translating GRI to ESRS.} Because only three firms report directly under the ESRS
(Mowi, Bolton, Espersen) and three under the GRI Standards (Nomad Foods, Profand,
Thai Union), a bridge was built to score all six on one ruler. Each GRI
disclosure was mapped to the ESRS topical DR(s) it addresses, drawing on the
published GRI--ESRS interoperability mapping (EFRAG, 2024) and adjudicated case by
case where the correspondence is only partial. Coverage is therefore
\emph{ESRS-aligned coverage in practice} --- each firm's disclosures, whatever
their native framework, expressed on the common ESRS topical structure --- and is
explicitly \textbf{not} a determination of formal legal ESRS compliance. The
mapping rests on analyst judgement, whose soundness is checked through the expert
review reported in Section~\ref{framework-validation-strategy}. %LI: what does this mean? + should we have a paragraph where we say why we map against the ESRS (and what it actually is? not sure it's sufficiently stated before (check intro and chapter 3))

\textbf{Coding and consolidation.} Coverage is coded as binary presence or absence
at DR level: a firm scores~1 on a requirement where its reporting contains a
disclosure mapping to that requirement, and~0 otherwise. This measures
\emph{breadth}, not depth, quality or assurance --- a requirement touched in a
single sentence and one reported exhaustively both score~1. The per-firm codings
were consolidated and analysed in a Jupyter notebook, which builds the
firm-by-requirement coverage matrix and the frequency distribution from which the
results in the following sections are drawn.

\textbf{The screened universe and the funnel.} The analysis begins from a screened
set of 70 ESRS topical DRs judged potentially relevant to seafood processing %LI: where do these specifically come from - this is set from EFRAG or just all DRs metnioned by cohort?;
this set is the denominator for every coverage figure in the chapter. Applying the
coding produces a natural narrowing: of the 70 screened DRs, 62 are reported by at
least one firm (the remaining eight, reported by none, are examined in
Section~\ref{cohort-shape}), 26 by at least five of the six, and 13 by all six.
This funnel --- 70 screened, 62 reported, 26 near-universal, 13 universal --- is
the structure the results then read for the height of the frontier, its universal
core, and its blind spots.

\textbf{What frequency does and does not mean.} One caveat governs the reading of
this funnel: it measures \emph{reporting convention} among large, mostly
CSRD-scoped reporters, not materiality for a small processor. A frequently
reported requirement is one an SME is likely to be \emph{asked about} as that
convention cascades down the value chain --- a demand-relevance proxy --- rather
than a topic asserted to be material for any individual firm. Whether these
revealed priorities fit a particular firm's situation is a separate question,
taken up in the cross-framework mapping and the worked case that follow.

\subsection{The height of the frontier: a coverage
gradient}\label{cohort-gradient}

Coverage ranges from 33 to 53 of the 70 screened DRs
(Figure~\ref{fig:p1-heatmap}). Thai Union is the single deepest reporter
at 53 DRs (76\%), followed by Bolton (45; 64\%) and Nomad Foods (42;
60\%); the median large reporter covers roughly 40 DRs, around 57\% of the
screened set. The immediate and consequential observation is that
\emph{even the frontier is incomplete}: the most advanced reporter in the
sector leaves around one relevant requirement in four uncovered, and the
median large reporter closer to two in five. %LI: what do you mean by this? make clearer 2 in 5?
If the sector's most
resourced firms do not disclose the full set of relevant requirements, the
realistic ceiling for a resource-constrained SME sits well below
completeness --- a point that reframes the SME question from ``how do we
report everything?'' to ``which subset matters most?'', which the demand
mapping in the following chapters answers.

The company-by-standard heatmap also shows that firms specialise: Bolton
is deepest on own-workforce disclosure (S1, 16 of 17), Espersen is thin on
S1 (4 of 17) but complete on consumers/end-users (S4, 5 of 5), and Mowi
reports nothing under affected communities (S3) or consumers (S4). The
frontier is therefore not only incomplete in height but uneven in shape
from firm to firm.

\subsection{The universal core: what every firm
reports}\label{cohort-core}

Beneath the gradient lies a stable core. Reading the universe by the
number of firms reporting each requirement produces a clean funnel: of the
62 DRs reported by at least one firm, 26 are reported by at least five, and
13 by \emph{all six} (Figure~\ref{fig:p1-frequency-r}). These 13
requirements are the sector's empirically revealed table stakes %LI: table stakes - what is that word? --- the %LI: the assumption is that this is xxx
disclosures every mature reporter makes irrespective of business model.
They fall into four blocks:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Climate (3):} energy consumption and mix (E1-5), gross
  Scope~1--3 and total GHG emissions (E1-6), and climate targets (E1-4).
\item \textbf{Water (3):} actions and resources (E3-2), targets (E3-3),
  and water consumption (E3-4).
\item \textbf{Governance (2):} business-conduct policies and corporate
  culture (G1-1), and prevention and detection of corruption and bribery
  (G1-3).
\item \textbf{Social (5):} own-workforce policies (S1-1), workforce
  characteristics (S1-6), health-and-safety metrics (S1-14), and, for
  value-chain workers, policies (S2-1) and remediation channels (S2-3).
\end{itemize}

The full narrowing --- 70 screened, 62 reported by at least one firm, 26
by at least five, 13 by all --- is itself the chapter's central
descriptive result: the sector does not disclose uniformly across a broad
front, but converges tightly onto a compact, cross-cutting backbone.

\subsection{The shape of the frontier: volume, consistency, and blind
spots}\label{cohort-shape}

Among the 62 reported requirements, disclosure is markedly social-heavy by
volume: 32 are Social, 25 Environmental, and 5 Governance, with the
own-workforce standard (S1) alone accounting for 17. This social weighting
holds within firms as well as across the universe: for every cohort
member, Social is the largest block of reported DRs and Governance the
smallest (Figure~\ref{fig:p1-esg}).

Volume, however, is not the same as consensus. Measuring each topic by the
mean number of firms reporting its requirements reveals where the sector
genuinely agrees (Figure~\ref{fig:p1-depth}): consistency is highest for
water (E3, mean 5.75 of 6), governance (G1, 5.2), resource use and waste
(E5, 4.6) and value-chain workers (S2, 4.4), and lowest for pollution (E2,
2.0), affected communities (S3, 2.6) and consumers/end-users (S4, 3.0).
The frontier is thus concentrated on climate, water, governance and
workforce topics and thins markedly across pollution, community and
consumer disclosure.

The eight requirements reported by no firm sharpen this picture rather
than complicate it. Four are the ``anticipated financial effects''
disclosures --- for pollution (E2-6), water (E3-5), biodiversity (E4-6)
and circular economy (E5-6) --- the forward-looking, financially
quantified requirements that are the most demanding and are phased in under
the standards. The remaining four are pollution policies (E2-1),
substances of concern (E2-5), internal carbon pricing (E1-8) and payment
practices (G1-6). The sector's clearest collective blind spot is therefore
the financial quantification of environmental impact, followed by
pollution disclosure --- a pattern consistent with the low consistency of
E2 across the cohort.%LI: should we benchmark tis against other sectors - they are indeed phasing in under ESRS and maybe just not applicable for our sector as of now?

\subsection{Robustness across business models and reporting
year}\label{cohort-robustness}

Because the cohort spans four business models, the core can be tested for
model dependence. The universal-core requirements are reported across all
four models --- wild-capture, aquaculture, integrated and
processing/brand --- so the backbone is not an artefact of any single
production system. Business model shapes the \emph{margins} of the
frontier, not its core: model-specific topics such as feed and animal
health (aquaculture) or wild-stock status (capture) appear in the long
tail, while the climate, water, governance and workforce core is shared.
%LI: i don't tget this section? explain better what you mean or want to show?!

Two further checks confirm the core is not an artefact of the sample. First,
\emph{threshold sensitivity}: the near-universal set contracts smoothly with the
cut --- 56 requirements are reported by at least two firms, 26 by at least five,
and 13 by all six --- so the $\geq$5-of-6 line sits on a gradient rather than a
cliff edge. Second, \emph{cohort-size invariance}: re-deriving the near-universal
set on the five European firms alone, dropping the non-European Thai Union at the
equivalent $\geq$4-of-5 cut, returns the \emph{identical} 26-requirement set --- a
complete overlap, so the backbone does not depend on which firms are in the
cohort. Thai Union itself, the single non-EU firm and the broadest reporter (53 of
70), reports \emph{more} widely on biodiversity, affected communities and
pollution --- consistent with the NGO and market scrutiny a global tuna major
faces --- yet only one requirement is reported uniquely by it, so the consensus
core survives the EU/non-EU divide as common ground rather than an
EU-regulatory artefact. %LI: again clearer language - what is the point of this paragraph?

The cohort also mixes 2024 and 2025 reporting years, and the three firms
for which both years are available show that disclosure is not expanding
monotonically. Between 2024 and 2025, reported coverage \emph{fell}
slightly for all three --- Nomad Foods from 43 to 42 DRs, Profand from 40
to 38, and Espersen from 36 to 34 --- with the change driven by a handful
of requirements dropped and very few added
(Figure~\ref{fig:p1-year}). The main analysis uses each firm's most recent
report, so these are the figures carried in Table~\ref{tab:cohort-r}. The
modest contraction is consistent with a reporting environment entering a
phase of consolidation and simplification rather than continued expansion,
a theme the discussion returns to in connection with the Omnibus

\subsection{What the cohort establishes --- and what it does
not}\label{cohort-limits}

The cohort establishes three things. It fixes the height of the sector's
disclosure frontier at 53 requirements for the deepest reporter and around
57\% of the screened set for the median large firm; it identifies a
13-requirement universal core spanning climate, water, governance and
workforce that constitutes the sector's de facto table stakes; and it
locates the sector's collective blind spots in the anticipated-financial-
effect and pollution requirements. Together these calibrate the realistic
disclosure ambition for a smaller firm and supply the empirical anchor for
the cross-framework demand mapping that follows.

Its limits are equally definite and are stated so that the findings are
not read beyond what they support. The cohort is a purposive sample of six
upper-bound reporters and is not representative of the sector, so no claim
about typical or average practice is made. Coverage is binary presence at
DR level and speaks to breadth, not to the depth, quality or assurance of
disclosure. The mapping from mixed ESRS and GRI source documents onto the
ESRS requirement set rests on analyst judgement, checked through the expert
review in Section~\ref{framework-validation-strategy}. And the analysis is a
2024--2025 snapshot of a rapidly moving reporting landscape. Within these
bounds, the cohort provides what it was designed to provide: a defensible,
document-grounded map of the seafood disclosure frontier.
