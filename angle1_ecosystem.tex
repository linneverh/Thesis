The first angle maps the \emph{ecosystem}: the full set of instruments that act
on a European seafood-processing SME, arranged by how obligatory each is and
which stakeholder drives it. This map establishes the columns of the demand
matrix used throughout the thesis, and, read on its own, already reveals the
shape of the burden the sector faces. The chapter first sets out how the
landscape was catalogued (Section~5.1); it then describes the composition of the
resulting demand environment (Section~5.2) and the single most consequential
pattern in it --- that direct law is narrow while breadth arrives by cascade
(Section~5.3).

\textbf{Organising frame.} Instruments are arrayed on two axes. The
\emph{necessity} axis ranks each instrument by how imperative it is from the
SME's perspective, in four tiers: Tier~1, legal licence to operate (binding
law); Tier~2, market licence to operate (customer-demanded certifications and
audits without which market access is lost); Tier~3, competitive expectation
(reporting frameworks peers adopt); and Tier~4, strategic differentiation
(voluntary leadership initiatives). %LI: explain better what each tier is - maybe we make link here with literature we previously put in chapter 3? it's applicable here and explains a bit why we have 4 tiers no?
The \emph{domain} axis separates seafood-sector-specific instruments from general corporate-sustainability
instruments. Two bands sit outside this grid: a Tier~0 food-integrity foundation
--- the food-safety and food-law floor every processor clears before
sustainability strategy begins, in scope of the firm but out of scope of the
disclosure analysis --- and an enabling layer of methods, management systems and
data infrastructure used to satisfy the demands above (the ``methodology
providers versus disclosure askers'' distinction, developed with the scoring
mechanics in Chapter~7).

\textbf{Inclusion rule.} An instrument/framework is admitted to the scored grid only if it
passes two gates, judged on the instrument's own text. \emph{Gate~1 (ESG
topic):} the instrument bears on an environmental, social or governance matter,
which excludes fiscal, customs and data-protection law. \emph{Gate~2 (own
binding organisation-level obligation):} the instrument itself imposes on the
firm a binding organisation-level obligation --- a disclosure, due-diligence,
certification, management-mechanism or market-access requirement --- rather than
an individual employee entitlement or an obligation that binds only Member
States. Gate~2 admits process-and-mechanism duties (the OSH Framework Directive,
the Whistleblowing Directive's internal-reporting-channel requirement) on the
same footing as reporting duties, while excluding individual-entitlement labour
law (the Work--Life Balance Directive) and Member-State-binding instruments (the
Adequate Minimum Wages Directive). %LI: this has to be clearer - not understandable. %LI: keep the notion of tier 0 out no added value, just confusion. A third carve-out keeps product- and
food-safety conformity in the Tier~0 foundation, even though the consumer-health
topic re-enters the demand mapping through the frameworks that ask about it. The
full candidate set --- including instruments considered and rejected, with the
gate each failed --- is recorded in a scope log, so that membership is
reproducible rather than a matter of recall.

\textbf{Stakeholder source and obligation locus.} Each admitted instrument
carries two attributes. Its \emph{source} records the dominant stakeholder whose
demand the processor actually answers, determined by the test ``does the
instrument legally bind the processor?'': where it does, the source is the
regulator; where it does not, the source is the transmitting stakeholder --- a
customer audit, a capital-market expectation, or an industry/NGO initiative. For
a sub-threshold SME selling B2B to large retailers, this rule reassigns several
instruments whose nominal issuer is a regulator to the customer channel through
which the SME actually experiences them: CSRD/ESRS, CSDDD, CDP and VSME are coded
\emph{customer}, because the dominant transmitter is the large customer's
value-chain data request rather than the capital market --- note that VSME exists precisely
to answer that request with one standard. The \emph{investor} code is reserved
for instruments whose primary intended user is capital (SFDR and IFRS~S1/S2). The
GDST traceability standard is left at \emph{industry/NGO}: unlike the MSC/ASC
certifications it is not yet a standing retailer requirement, so its demand is
not yet transmitted through the customer channel. Secondly, the \emph{obligation locus}
records separately whether the processor is the bound entity (\emph{direct}), is
reached through another party's duty (\emph{indirect}), or merely operates within
a regime that binds someone else (\emph{context}). %LI: give an example otherwise vague. 

\textbf{Firm-profile conditionality.} Several instruments apply only to firms
with particular characteristics. These are flagged scope-conditional and
governed by firm-profile include-switches: the F-gas Regulation applies only
where fluorinated refrigerants are used; the Pay Transparency Directive only at
or above 150 employees; ISSF only to tuna processors. Setting the firm profile
activates or deactivates these columns and excludes the inapplicable ones from
that firm's demand sums. %LI: this should be part of angle 3 I think - not yet specify here - move!

\textbf{Output.} The catalogue records \textbf{38} instruments in total.
\textbf{Thirty} of these pass both gates and are actively scored, forming
\textbf{31 scored columns} --- the VSME standard is split into its Basic and
Comprehensive modules, adding one column over its single instrument --- each
tagged with its tier, domain, source, obligation locus and any firm-profile
condition. The remaining \textbf{eight} are carried in the catalogue for
ecosystem completeness but are not scored, because they impose no own
organisation-level obligation on the processor: the EU Taxonomy (whose
activity-eligibility gate returns null for the focal seafood-processing
activity, NACE~C10.20, and which is retained only as a project-conditional
finance lens); Fishery Improvement Projects (whose reporting binds the project,
not the processor); the UN SDGs (an aspirational goal framework with no corporate
reporting mechanism); the ``Move to $-15\,^{\circ}$C'' decarbonisation coalition;
two investor benchmarks that rate only large listed firms (the Coller~FAIRR
Protein Producer Index and the Dow~Jones Sustainability Index); a
keystone-company coalition the SME cannot join (SeaBOS); and a
scheme-benchmarking body that imposes no direct obligation on the processor
(GSSI). The map is intentionally \emph{not} mutually exclusive: a single topic
reached by several instruments remains visible under each, because the
cross-framework convergence measured in Chapter~7 depends on that overlap being
preserved rather than collapsed.

\subsection{5.2 Composition of the demand
environment}\label{ecosystem-composition}

% CL: findings begin — from the Results "Composition" subsection; profile = 50--250 employees, non-tuna.
The composition below is read for the modelled sub-threshold seafood-processing
SME (50--250 employees, not a tuna processor). Its distribution across the four
stakeholder channels is uneven (Figure~\ref{fig:r-actor-tier}): the environment
is dominated by regulators (12 columns) and customers (11), which together
account for 23 of the 31 columns (74\%), while investors are the thinnest channel
(3) and industry/NGO initiatives the remainder (5). The necessity tiers fall
12/7/8/4 across Tiers~1--4, so the regulatory legal-licence layer is the single
largest block. Four instruments are firm-profile \emph{opt-ins} --- the F-gas
Regulation, the Whistleblowing and Pay Transparency Directives, and ISSF ---
which belong to the scoring universe but switch on only when the firm meets their
trigger.

The matrix is sparse by construction: of the 992 instrument$\times$topic cells,
181 (18\%) carry a demand, and these split almost evenly between \emph{binding}
requirements (92 cells scoring~2) and \emph{touched} references (89 cells
scoring~1). The demand environment is therefore not a dense web in which every
framework touches every topic, but a structured field in which a minority of
concentrated, mostly regulator- and customer-borne demands define the landscape.

% CL: the tier/source correlation observation, kept brief and flagged for the discussion.
One structural pattern is worth noting for the discussion. Source and necessity
tier are coded on independent criteria, yet the two axes correlate: the
instruments at Tier~4 (strategic differentiation) cluster almost entirely in the
\emph{industry/NGO} channel (SBTi, the UN Global Compact, voluntary coalitions),
while the binding licence-to-operate tiers are dominated by the \emph{regulator}
channel. Strategic, proactively-adopted initiatives tend to be NGO- or
coalition-run rather than regulator-mandated or capital-demanded; this is
developed further in the discussion.

\subsection{5.3 Narrow law, broad cascade}\label{ecosystem-narrow-broad}

% CL: promoted your key finding to its own subsection so it is unmissable.
A further pattern concerns not \emph{who} demands but how \emph{broadly} each
instrument demands, and it reframes the problem. Individual regulations are
narrow: each of the twelve regulatory instruments touches on average only two
topics, and none more than three --- the Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation
covers waste and packaging, the occupational-health directive covers health and
safety, the F-gas Regulation covers emissions. Breadth --- and therefore the
overlap that creates the SME's burden --- comes overwhelmingly from the market
and voluntary side: customer instruments demand on average nine topics each (the
CSDDD eighteen), and industry and NGO reporting frameworks almost eight (GRI
alone twenty-five).

The wall of overlapping demand is therefore not the product of the SME being
directly over-regulated. It is the product of a few \emph{broad} horizontal
instruments --- CSRD, CSDDD, SFDR --- that apply to the firm's large customers
and financiers and \emph{cascade} down the value chain as supplier
questionnaires, together with the voluntary certification and rating landscape.
Direct regulation on the processor is targeted and lean; the breadth is
transmitted, not imposed. This carries a policy corollary, developed in the
discussion: because the burden originates in the downward cascade of large-firm
regulation rather than in narrow direct rules, it is the coordination or capping
of that cascade --- precisely the aim of the VSME and the Omnibus value-chain
provisions --- that would most reduce it.

This angle yields a map, not yet a priority. It shows who asks and how bindingly,
and it locates the burden in the cascade of broad horizontal regulation rather
than in direct rules. What it cannot show is which of these many demanded topics
a firm should build \emph{first}. That requires two further steps: establishing
what the sector's most mature firms actually report --- the realistic frontier of
seafood disclosure (Chapter~6) --- and then crossing that frontier with the
ecosystem mapped here to locate where demand converges (Chapter~7).
