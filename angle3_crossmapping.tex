\section{7. Cross-framework demand mapping: where to build
first}\label{angle3-crossmapping}

This is the chapter the thesis has been building toward. Angles~1 and~2 gathered
the raw materials --- who demands what, and what mature firms actually report ---
but neither, on its own, tells a resource-limited firm what to \emph{do}. This
angle turns the two into a decision %LI: rather foundation for a decision no?: a short, ranked list of the disclosure topics
a firm should build first, and a diagnostic that tailors that list to the firm's
own situation. That is the thesis's central practical contribution, and it is
produced here.

The first two angles supply the two dimensions of a single instrument. Angle~1
(Chapter~5) fixed the \emph{columns} --- the instruments that make up the
disclosure ecosystem and how binding each is. Angle~2 (Chapter~6) fixed the
\emph{rows} --- the disclosure topics the sector's leading firms actually report,
narrowed to a cohort-evidenced universe. This chapter crosses the two. It scores,
topic by topic and instrument by instrument, \emph{who demands what}, and reads
the resulting matrix for the question the whole thesis exists to answer: given a
fragmented wall of overlapping demands, which topics, if built first, satisfy the
most of them. Section~7.1 sets out the convergence logic that makes this a
tractable question; Sections~7.2--7.4 build, score and stage the demand matrix;
Sections~7.5--7.7 report what it shows; and Sections~7.8--7.9 turn the finding
into a datapoint layer, a diagnostic tool, and a worked case.

\subsection{7.1 The convergence logic}\label{cm-theory}

The move from a wall of demands to a short build-first list rests on one idea:
that the demands, though issued by different actors in different vocabularies,
draw on a much smaller set of underlying capabilities, so that a single
capability can answer many demands at once. Two established literatures make this
precise. The first is the \emph{resource-based view} of the firm and the
dynamic-capabilities tradition (Barney, 1991; Teece, 2007), which treat the
ability to produce and reuse structured data as an organisational capability that,
once built, is redeployable across uses at low marginal cost. The second is
\emph{stakeholder-salience} theory (Mitchell, Agle \& Wood, 1997): a firm facing
many legitimate claimants cannot serve each separately and must triage, and the
rational triage is toward the topics on which the most salient claimants converge.

Convergence is therefore the organising construct. Where a topic is demanded by a
regulator, an investor framework and an industry standard alike, capability built for that topic is not spent once but several
times over --- the compound return that distinguishes a proactive posture from a
reactive one (Chapter~1). A topic demanded through only one channel yields a
narrower return. The analytical task of this chapter is thus to \emph{measure}
convergence across the fragmented landscape and to identify the topics where it is
highest --- the hotspots at which building once satisfies many.

\subsection{7.3 Scoring the matrix}\label{cm-matrix}

\textbf{The coverage scale.} Each cell is coded on a three-level scale defined ex
ante:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{0 --- not covered:} the instrument does not address the topic, or its
demand maps only to datapoints outside the row universe (the out-of-frame rule
below).
\item \textbf{1 --- touched:} the instrument references the topic but imposes no
binding requirement on any of its datapoints.
\item \textbf{2 --- required:} the instrument explicitly and bindingly requires
the entity to achieve, address, comply with, certify, report on or disclose at
least one datapoint belonging to the topic.
\end{itemize}
Binding \emph{conformity} counts equally with binding \emph{disclosure}: a
mandatory recycled-content target (PPWR), an accident-prevention duty (OSH) or a
market-access prohibition on forced-labour products scores~2 even where no report
is filed, because it bindingly addresses the datapoint substance. The score
measures the \emph{strength} of demand and is deliberately independent of
obligation locus: an instrument that reaches the processor indirectly --- CSDDD,
cascaded down from large customers --- can still score~2 where it bindingly
imposes a topic's datapoint substance. Locus is recorded as a separate attribute
and never raises or lowers the cell score. (%LI:? what do you mean - not clear?)

\textbf{Datapoint-grounded coding.} A score is assigned against the
\emph{constituent datapoints} of a requirement, not against the title of its
disclosure requirement. Each of the 28 cohort-evidenced rows is decomposed into
its official EFRAG datapoints using the ESRS datapoint master (Commission
Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/2772): the 26 DR-level rows resolve to 356
datapoints and the two topic-level rows (E4, S4) to a further 152. An instrument
scores~2 where its indicators demand at least one of a requirement's datapoints,
1 where it references the topic but demands none of them, and~0 where it is
silent. This converts the loose question ``does instrument~$f$ address topic~$r$?''
into the concrete, reproducible ``does~$f$ demand any datapoint of~$r$?'', and it
surfaces corrections --- whistleblowing demand resolving to the governance row
that actually carries whistleblowing datapoints rather than an adjacent
anti-corruption row --- that, once logged, are findings about coding rather than
matters of method.

\textbf{Self-assessment versus binding demand.} The score turns on whether the
instrument imposes the datapoint's substance, not on who reports it. An instrument
resting on \emph{voluntary self-assessment} --- where the entity self-describes
against the instrument's own open questions, with no externally defined
performance threshold and no standardised binding disclosure --- evidences that the
topic is in scope (score~1) but does not impose the datapoint (so does not reach
2). This caps open-question ratings and questionnaires (EcoVadis, the Sedex SAQ)
at~1. A~2 requires one of three things: an enforced conformity bar the entity must
meet (a SMETA audit whose non-conformities must be remediated to retain the
buyer); reporting against a standardised, datapoint-defined disclosure standard,
whether voluntary (GRI, CDP, TNFD) or mandatory (the ESRS); or a binding legal
obligation imposing the datapoint's substance. The distinction is between an
\emph{open-question} rating, where the entity self-describes against loose prompts
(capped at~1), and a \emph{defined-datapoint} standard, where the datapoints to be
reported are fixed (which can reach~2). Standardised disclosure standards score~2
even though they too are self-reported, because their datapoints are defined, not
self-framed. A related distinction separates a \emph{rating} from a
\emph{standard}: a rating is admitted only where the firm must itself supply data
into it (EcoVadis, scored at~1 because the processor completes it), whereas purely
external ratings built from public data (MSCI, Sustainalytics, the Dow~Jones
Sustainability Index) impose no own obligation and are not scored --- a rating
consumes disclosures rather than defining them.

\textbf{Sector-material rows.} For the four seafood-specific gap rows
(traceability, wild-stock status, feed and marine ingredients, animal health and
welfare) no ESRS datapoint exists by construction; these are scored against the
criteria of the demanding instruments themselves --- MSC Principle~1 stock-status
reference points for wild-stock, the GDST key data elements for traceability, and
ASC antibiotic and survival criteria for animal health and welfare. A pinned rule
prevents double-counting on the wild-stock/E4 boundary: stock-status and
legal-sourcing demand (MSC Principle~1, the IUU catch-certificate regime,
FisheryProgress) codes to the wild-stock row, whereas generic biodiversity and
ecosystem-impact demand (ESRS~E4, GRI~101, TNFD, MSC Principle~2 on bycatch and
habitat) codes to E4; a single instrument may legitimately score on both rows
through different provisions.

\textbf{Certification coding via chain of custody.} A processor holds only the
\emph{Chain-of-Custody} (CoC) certification of a scheme (MSC, ASC, GLOBALG.A.P.,
BAP, Friend of the Sea); it is not itself assessed against the upstream Fisheries
or Farm Standard. But the binding precondition for holding the CoC is that it
source product certified to that upstream standard. Each topic is therefore scored
against what the scheme bindingly requires, at the depth it requires it: a core
criterion (MSC~P1 stock status, ASC feed-conversion and effluent limits,
GFSI-recognised food safety) scores~2, a lighter good-practice mention~1. What the
processor holds --- the CoC, and for BAP's plant standard its own plant --- is
\emph{direct}; the upstream stock, ecosystem, feed and welfare performance it
sources is \emph{indirect}.

\textbf{Out-of-frame demand.} Some indicator-level demand maps cleanly onto an
ESRS datapoint that lies \emph{outside} the cohort-defined universe: the Pay
Transparency Directive's gender pay-gap requirement maps to S1-16, the Working
Time Directive to S1-11/S1-15, none of which cleared the $\geq$5/6 consensus and
so have no row. Such demand scores~0 against the in-universe rows and is
\emph{not} reassigned to the nearest available row (which would misattribute and
inflate it); instead it is recorded in a separate out-of-frame register
(instrument~$\rightarrow$ demanded DR~$\rightarrow$ reason). A framework's
contribution to the matrix is thus the \emph{intersection} of its demand with the
cohort-evidenced universe, not the union of everything it asks. That mandatory
labour-standards disclosure and the voluntary reporting universe occupy partly
disjoint spaces is itself a finding for the discussion.

\textbf{Methodology providers versus disclosure askers.} Instruments that supply
methods but do not themselves pressure an entity to report --- the GHG Protocol,
ISO~14001 --- are held in an enabling layer and excluded from the demand sums,
distinct from the disclosure askers (CDP, SBTi, MSC) that require an entity to
submit or certify information. Every non-zero cell is documented in a
justification log with a one-line rationale, a citation to the source clause or
indicator, and the identifier(s) of the ESRS datapoint(s) the demand maps to ---
the audit trail that protects the coding against challenge. Figure~\ref{fig:cell-coding}
sets out the decision applied to each cell. %LI: the excell i was talking about (phase 3) maybe we should add to appendix?

\subsection{7.4 Hotspot measures and stage
assignment}\label{cm-staging}

With the matrix populated, the columns are collapsed into the four
stakeholder-driver channels (regulator, customer, investor, industry/NGO), and two
complementary measures are computed for every topic:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Breadth ($\Sigma$)} --- the sum of the topic's coverage scores across
all active instruments, so a binding requirement (2) counts double a touch (1). It
measures the \emph{volume} of demand. %LI: explain simpler!
\item \textbf{Convergence (0--4)} --- the number of the four stakeholder channels
in which at least one instrument demands the topic. Because it counts
\emph{channels}, not instruments, it is robust by construction to within-channel
repetition (five overlapping certifications collapse to a single customer signal)
and captures cross-stakeholder consensus rather than sheer volume.
\end{itemize}
Convergence is the primary signal and breadth the secondary one: convergence is
the dedup-robust measure of how widely the fragmented landscape agrees on a topic,
while breadth --- which overlapping instruments can inflate --- discriminates
between topics of equal convergence.

Topics are then triaged into three stages on the two measures jointly:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stage~1 --- build first:} convergence $\geq 3$ \emph{and} breadth
$\geq 12$. Demanded across at least three of the four channels \emph{and} in the
top quartile of demand volume --- the no-regret core, where capability built for
one channel serves several.
\item \textbf{Stage~2 --- next:} convergence $\geq 3$ but breadth below the
quartile cut. Broad consensus, less intense demand; build after the core.
\item \textbf{Stage~3 --- monitor:} convergence $\leq 2$. Demanded through only one
or two channels; required for compliance where in scope, but not yet drawing broad
demand.
\end{itemize}
Both thresholds are anchored in the empirical distribution rather than set
arbitrarily (Figure~\ref{fig:topic-distribution}). The convergence cut of three is
the simple majority of the four channels. The breadth cut of twelve is the
\emph{third quartile} of the breadth distribution across the 32 topics, so the
Stage-1 volume gate selects the top quartile and recalibrates automatically if the
scores change. In the modelled firm no topic below convergence three reaches the
breadth cut, so the two criteria identify one coherent Stage-1 set rather than two
competing ones.

\subsection{7.5 The composition of the demand
environment}\label{cm-composition}

The populated matrix is read first as a description of the demand
\emph{environment} the modelled sub-threshold SME (50--250 employees, non-tuna)
faces. Its 31 scored columns are distributed unevenly across the four channels
(Figure~\ref{fig:r-actor-tier}): the environment is dominated by regulators (12
columns) and customers (11), which together account for 23 of the 31 columns
(74\%), while investors are the thinnest channel (3) and industry/NGO initiatives
the remainder (5). The necessity tiers fall 12/7/8/4 across Tiers~1--4, so the
regulatory legal-licence layer is the single largest block.

The matrix is sparse by construction: of the 992 instrument$\times$topic cells,
181 (18\%) carry a demand, split almost evenly between \emph{binding} requirements
(92 cells scoring~2) and \emph{touched} references (89 cells scoring~1). The demand
environment is therefore not a dense web in which every framework touches every
topic, but a structured field in which a minority of concentrated, mostly
regulator- and customer-borne demands define the landscape.

One structural pattern is worth noting for the discussion. Source and necessity
tier are coded on independent criteria, yet the two axes correlate: the
instruments at Tier~4 (strategic differentiation) cluster almost entirely in the
industry/NGO channel (SBTi, the UN Global Compact, voluntary coalitions), while
the binding licence-to-operate tiers are dominated by the regulator channel.
Strategic, proactively-adopted initiatives tend to be NGO- or coalition-run rather
than regulator-mandated or capital-demanded --- an early reading of where in the
landscape demand is obligatory versus elective. %LI: this makes sense because they are not mandatory yet - stupid comment no?

\subsection{7.6 Narrow law, broad cascade}\label{cm-narrow-broad}

A second pattern concerns not \emph{who} demands but how \emph{broadly} each
instrument demands, and it reframes the SME's problem. Individual regulations are
narrow: each of the twelve regulatory instruments touches on average only two
topics, and none more than three --- the Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation
covers waste and packaging, the occupational-health directive covers health and
safety, the F-gas Regulation covers emissions. Breadth --- and therefore the
overlap that creates the SME's burden --- comes overwhelmingly from the market and
voluntary side: customer instruments demand on average nine topics each (the CSDDD
eighteen), and industry and NGO reporting frameworks almost eight (GRI alone
twenty-five).

The wall of overlapping demand is therefore not the product of the SME being
directly over-regulated. It is the product of a few \emph{broad} horizontal
instruments --- CSRD, CSDDD, SFDR --- that apply to the firm's large customers and
financiers and \emph{cascade} down the value chain as supplier questionnaires,
together with the voluntary certification and rating landscape. Direct regulation
on the processor is targeted and lean; the breadth is transmitted, not imposed.
This carries a policy corollary, developed in the discussion: because the burden
originates in the downward cascade of large-firm regulation rather than in narrow
direct rules, it is the coordination or capping of that cascade --- precisely the
aim of the VSME and the Omnibus value-chain provisions --- that would most reduce
it.

\subsection{7.7 The build-first set by production
system}\label{cm-build-first}

Because several seafood instruments and topics are production-system-specific, the
diagnostic returns a different Stage-1 (build-first) set depending on whether the
firm is wild-capture, aquaculture, or both (Figure~\ref{fig:r-stage-system}). For
the standard modelled profile the Stage-1 set is nine topics for a mixed-sourcing
firm, eight for an aquaculture-only firm, and five for a wild-capture-only firm.

The \emph{overlap} is a universal core of four topics that should be build-first under
every production system: energy (E1-5), greenhouse-gas emissions (E1-6),
biodiversity and ecosystems (E4), and supply-chain traceability. These are the
no-regret priorities for any European seafood processor --- demanded broadly,
across stakeholder channels, irrespective of business model.

The \emph{differences} are structured rather than arbitrary. Four further topics
--- water (E3-4), waste (E5-5), occupational health and safety (S1-14) and
value-chain workers (S2-1) --- are Stage~1 for mixed and aquaculture firms but slip
to Stage~2 for a wild-capture firm. This is a volume effect, not a change in
importance: the matrix carries three aquaculture certifications (ASC, BAP,
GLOBALG.A.P.) against two wild certifications (MSC, Friend of the Sea), so a
wild-only firm loses the certification demand that lifts these shared topics over
the breadth threshold, even though their cross-stakeholder convergence is unchanged
and several retain a regulatory floor (OSH, the Forced Labour Regulation, PPWR).
The remaining divergence is in the sector-gap rows: wild-stock status is
build-relevant only to wild and mixed firms, while feed and animal
health/antimicrobial use apply only to aquaculture and mixed firms. The
personalisation therefore refines \emph{which} of a shared backbone an individual
firm should prioritise, rather than producing wholly different agendas for
different firms.

\subsection{7.8 From themes to datapoints, and the diagnostic
tool}\label{cm-datapoints-tool}

\textbf{The build-first data layer.} The staging returns priority \emph{themes};
a firm still needs to know which \emph{datapoints} to collect for each. A
datapoint layer translates every priority theme into the specific data the firm
must produce, assembled from five sources. The ESRS topical requirements decompose
into their constituent datapoints (the ESRS datapoint catalogue under Commission
Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/2772). For each theme the \emph{proportionate}
subset --- the realistic starting point for a smaller firm --- is identified
through the VSME-to-ESRS crosswalk, so datapoints inside the VSME Basic or
Comprehensive modules are marked ``start-here'' and the remainder as depth to add
when a specific demand requires it. The four seafood-specific themes with no ESRS
requirement (wild-stock, feed, animal health/antimicrobial use, traceability) are
sourced instead from the GRI~13 sector standard. Each theme is paired with the
enabling method that produces its data (the GHG Protocol for emissions, GDST for
traceability), drawn from the enabling layer; and each datapoint carries the count
of stakeholder demands it serves, taken from the matrix, which makes the reuse
explicit --- a single datapoint typically satisfies several frameworks at once.
Because both the ESRS and the VSME are under active Omnibus revision, the layer is
a dated snapshot documented with its sources. (should we again refer to excell that makes crosswalk ESRS to VMSE in appendix - or do you not add excellss to appendix?)

\textbf{The diagnostic tool.} The tool operationalises the staging logic for an
individual SME through two inputs and two outputs.
\emph{Input~1 --- reporting-posture self-assessment:} a short structured
self-assessment (5--8 items) positioning the firm on the reactive-to-proactive
spectrum (formal sustainability strategy; a dedicated function or role; whether
reporting is externally or internally initiated; voluntary commitments such as
SBTi-aligned targets; whether disclosure data is reusable across requests).
\emph{Input~2 --- stakeholder-pressure mapping:} the firm identifies which
stakeholders are actively requesting information (which retail customers and
questionnaire formats, which certifications are required for placement, any
investor or beyond-baseline regulatory pressure), reflecting its actual external
demand environment.
\emph{Output~1 --- prioritised topics:} the subset of Stage-1 topics whose
demanding frameworks align with the firm's identified pressures --- where
capability investment delivers maximum response coverage.
\emph{Output~2 --- staged roadmap:} a recommended sequencing of capability
investment across Stage~1 $\rightarrow$ Stage~2 $\rightarrow$ Stage~3, scaled to
the firm's posture and resourcing. Positioning on the reactive-to-proactive
spectrum is left to the self-assessment on the basis that the posture is a
behavioural rather than a measurable categorical state, and the firm is best
placed to characterise its own orientation.

\subsection{7.9 Worked case and validation}\label{cm-worked-case}

\textbf{Worked case.} The framework is applied to a worked case constructed from
the public disclosures of a representative European mid-sized seafood-processing
SME (50--250 employees, primarily a B2B supplier to large EU retailers). The case
uses only material the firm has itself published --- its sustainability report,
certification listings, and customer and product information --- so the application
is fully reproducible and depends on no privileged access. The firm's profile is
characterised from public sources; the diagnostic is run on that profile; and its
output is examined for correspondence to the firm's observable position: whether
it locates the firm's posture plausibly and whether the topics it flags as
build-first are consistent with the demands the firm is visibly subject to. The
case is intentionally singular and \emph{formative rather than confirmatory} --- a
usability demonstration of the diagnostic, not a test of the underlying empirical
claims --- and no generalisation beyond it is claimed; practitioner validation with
the firm itself is left to future work.

\textbf{Validation of the mapping and the mechanism.} The demand mapping and the
capability-sharing claim it rests on are substantiated in a deliberately
document-based way, so that credibility derives from convergent, independently
inspectable evidence rather than any single informant. The \emph{descriptive}
claim --- that the ecosystem can be organised exhaustively and unambiguously by the
necessity, domain and source axes --- is tested in Angle~1 through the completeness
of the classification and its expert review by sustainability leads at the cohort
firms (member checking; Lincoln \& Guba, 1985). The \emph{explanatory} claim ---
that capabilities are shared across requirements, so that investment compelled by a
lower-tier requirement subsidises higher-tier disclosure --- is substantiated here
through an \emph{input-overlap analysis}: for each shared capability, the data and
process inputs required by the instruments that draw on it are read directly from
their published specifications. That MSC chain-of-custody, the GDST standard and
ESRS value-chain disclosure all draw on a common body of lot-level traceability
data is document-verifiable evidence that the capability is genuinely shared, not
self-reported. The defensible position is that the evidence is \emph{consistent
with} the capability-sharing mechanism --- the instruments demonstrably share
inputs --- rather than that a causal relationship is proven; retrospective
practitioner testimony on whether capability built for a mandatory requirement
reduced the marginal effort of later disclosures is the natural next stage of
validation, and is left to future work.

\medskip
What this angle delivers is the thesis's central practical output: not a map of who
asks (Angle~1) or of what mature firms report (Angle~2), but a ranked, personalised
answer to \emph{where a resource-limited firm should build first} --- a compact
Stage-1 core, a datapoint layer beneath it, and a diagnostic that tailors both to a
firm's own situation. The implications for seafood SMEs and for EU disclosure
policy are drawn out in the discussion that follows.
